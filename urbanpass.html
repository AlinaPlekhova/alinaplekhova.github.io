/ 01about

UrbanPass is a mobile platform that brings AIoT to urban infrastructure — from smart parking to EV charging. It works with real estate managers, parking operators, and car manufacturers globally.

My role: Lead product designer
Timeline: 6 weeks (discovery + design)
What I did: user flows, component library, wireframes, UI, and research alongside the PM

/ 02problem definition

With the rapid rise of EV adoption, UrbanPass users and parking operators face growing challenges.

EV adoption is rising fast, and both drivers and parking operators are feeling the strain. The PM and I ran discovery together to map the real friction around EV infrastructure and set a foundation for future features.

/ gathering insights

I ran interviews and surveys with two groups:

  • EV drivers — struggling to locate compatible chargers, decode unclear parking policies, and deal with broken kiosks.
  • Parking operators — juggling hybrid parking models, inconsistent user behaviour, and underused smart tech.
Image — key use cases table
/ problem statement worksheet

I built a collaborative worksheet and facilitated the team through turning raw feedback into clear problem statements, clustered into three themes: unauthorised parking, kiosk payment friction, and congestion at charging stations.

The worksheet pushed us toward sharper questions:

  • Why can't drivers reserve a bay in advance, even when they'd happily pay for it?
  • What would it take to remove kiosks entirely and move everything into the app?
  • How do we identify repeat offenders without on-site staff?
Image — problem statement board
/ brainstorming & user flow mapping

I facilitated co-creation sessions and reverse journey-mapping with the team — mapping existing flows step by step, then hunting for the gaps.

Key questions we explored:

  • How might a driver reserve a bay and start charging in a few taps?
  • How do we minimise no-shows without punishing honest drivers?
  • What's the interaction between the physical parking lock and the app?
Image — user flow map

The goal was to make the EV experience app-first and effortless

/ 03user flow with system states

I mapped system responses as carefully as user actions — bay lock/unlock, camera triggers, API calls, timeouts. A slow camera or a dropped API call shouldn't break the experience, so every step got a fallback and an error state.

Image — user flow with system states

This is the layer most reservation flows skip, and it's where the product either holds up in the real world or falls apart.

/ 04wireframes

I built reusable components for every parking and charging state: available, reserved, occupied, out of order. Figma variants and auto-layout meant the dev team could pull components without coming back to me.

What I personally designed: the component library for bay states, the reservation flow, the payment sheet UI, and error states for API failures.

outcome

I don't have hard conversion metrics — the PM owned tracking and reporting. What I can share:

  • Dev team: the component library was "easy to use, no clarifications needed."
  • Stakeholders: the flow was "much clearer than before."
  • The PM reported a drop in support tickets about charger availability.
what I'd do differently
  • Metrics from day one. I'd align with the PM on success measures before starting — time to book, reservation completion rate, support ticket volume.
  • Basic accessibility checks. At minimum, colour contrast on payment buttons and focus states for keyboard users.

Tools: Figma · Notion · pen & paper for early flows
